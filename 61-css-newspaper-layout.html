The Daily Mirage

Study Reveals That Plants Prefer Jazz Over Classical

Botanists report improved growth with bass solos, leafy disapproval of violins.

A ten-year study at Greendale University has concluded that common houseplants exhibit “clear musical preferences.” While jazz and funk led to “lush, vibrant growth,” classical music resulted in “lethargic leaf-droop,” and heavy metal triggered “aggressive vine-spreading.” Lead researcher Dr. Flora Stemmons cautioned, “We’re not saying your fern has opinions. But if you play Coltrane, it might thank you.”

Town Council Votes to Replace All Roundabouts with Tiny Labyrinths

Citizens encouraged to “bring a compass and a sense of purpose.”

In a unanimous decision that one councilor described as “either visionary or deeply confusing,” the town of Pleasant Oaks has approved a plan to replace all traffic roundabouts with decorative hedge labyrinths. The move is intended to promote mindfulness, reduce speeding, and provide “a delightful weekend activity for families.” Critics warn that commuters may become trapped for hours, while supporters insist the real journey is the one within. Construction begins next Tuesday, pending delivery of 10,000 boxwoods and a ceremonial Minotaur statue.

Local Man Claims His Cat Is Writing a Memoir

Publishers reportedly “intrigued but suspicious.”

Resident Jeremy Finch told reporters that his tabby, Mr. Whiskerson, has been “tapping out chapters” on his keyboard every night. “It’s mostly about napping, the tyranny of closed doors, and my inferior hunting skills,” Finch explained. Several literary agents have expressed interest, though one noted that the prose “leans heavily into tuna metaphors.” Mr. Whiskerson could not be reached for comment, but was seen napping atop the manuscript’s outline.

Coffee Shop Introduys “Ambiguity Blend”

Customers report tasting “notes of maybe-berry, hint of regret, and possibly oak.”

The trendy café Brewed Awakening has launched a new coffee described only as “a mystery in a cup.” Baristas refuse to reveal the origin, roast, or flavor profile, saying the experience is “about the journey, not the tasting notes.” Early reviews are mixed: one patron called it “enigmatic yet overpriced,” while another claimed it “tastes like the answer to a question I didn’t ask.”

Lost City of Atlantis Rumored to Have Excellent Public Transport

Ancient scrolls suggest efficient trams, reliable ferry service.

Recently “discovered” tablets, currently in the possession of an anonymous collector, describe Atlantis not only as a sunken utopia but also as a “commuter’s paradise.” The texts praise its punctual seahorse-drawn carriages, well-marked coral-cycle paths, and “surprisingly reasonable” underwater tolls. Historians remain skeptical, though many agree that “if it did exist, their public transit system would put ours to shame.”
